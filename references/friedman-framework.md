# Jared Friedman's Startup Idea Framework (YC)
 
Source: distilled from Jared Friedman's YC talk "How to Get Startup Ideas." Use this as the explicit evaluation instrument in Stage 3 of the ideation workflow.
 
## The 4 most common mistakes
 
1. **Solution in search of a problem (CISP).** Starting from a technology or capability ("AI is cool, what could I apply it to?") and searching for a problem to justify it, rather than starting from a real problem people already care about. Diagnostic question: if you removed the technology, would anyone still describe this as a problem they have? If not, it's a CISP.
2. **Tar-pit ideas.** A widespread, superficially-easy-looking problem that has attracted founders for years (sometimes decades) without anyone solving it — because there's a real structural reason it's hard, not because no one's tried. Tar pits are dangerous specifically because they're *plausible*: you can picture the product easily, which is exactly why so many founders have wasted months on the same idea before you.
   - Diagnostic: Google the idea. Find who has tried it before (there will be someone). Talk to them if possible. Identify the *specific* mechanism that killed prior attempts — not a vague "it's hard," but the actual structural reason (e.g., "no incentive to maintain shared data," "unit economics don't survive at required accuracy," "the regulator won't allow full automation").
   - Tar pits are not necessarily unsolvable forever — but they require either a genuine new unlock (new technology, new regulation, new distribution) or a reframing that removes the specific mechanism, not just cosmetic differentiation.
3. **Wrong point on the pick-vs-wait spectrum.** Some founders grab the first idea that occurs to them with zero evaluation; others wait indefinitely for a "perfect" idea that doesn't exist. The healthy middle: pick an idea that's a good *starting point* — one with enough of the right properties that it can morph in a good direction — rather than optimizing for a final answer up front. No idea survives first contact with customers unchanged.
4. **Abstract, not tractable problems.** Founders sometimes respond to "find a real problem" by picking something huge and abstract (e.g., "global poverty") instead of something specific and tractable enough for a startup to actually attack. Bigness of the problem statement is not the same as bigness of the addressable market — look for something concrete enough to build a wedge into.
## The 10 evaluation questions
 
Run every serious candidate idea through all ten, explicitly:
 
1. **Do you have founder-market fit?** Is this team obviously, almost unfairly, the right team for this idea? (Reference case: PlanGrid — one founder had deep construction-industry knowledge, the other was the right engineer for an iPad-native product; the pairing was obviously correct.) Score fit honestly on a spectrum from "built this exact thing professionally" through "adjacent industry experience" to "no particular connection" — don't round adjacency up to fit.
2. **How big is the market?** Needs to be large — either big now, or currently small but clearly and rapidly growing (reference case: Coinbase in 2012, when Bitcoin trading was tiny but the trajectory was visible). Watch for markets that are large only in a vague, aggregated sense ("all companies") with no specific reachable buyer.
3. **How acute is the problem?** The sharpest test: if the alternative to your solution is literally *nothing* — not a worse competitor, actually nothing — that's about as acute as it gets (reference case: Brex, where startups literally could not get a corporate card before Brex existed). Most real ideas won't clear this exact bar, but the closer to "nothing" the alternative is, the stronger the idea.
4. **Do you have competition?** Existing competition is usually a *good* sign, not a bad one — it confirms the market pays for solutions. The real question is whether you have a **new insight** competitors are missing (reference case: Dropbox was the 20th cloud storage company, but had a specific technical insight — OS-level file sync instead of manual browser upload — that the other 19 lacked). Competition without a stated insight is a red flag; insight without competition often means no one wants it yet (see mistake #2's cousin: "no competitors" can mean "no market," not "untapped opportunity").
5. **Do you personally want this, or know people who do?** If the answer to both is genuinely no, that's a signal to go talk to real users before going further — not disqualifying on its own, but a flag.
6. **Why now?** Has something recently changed — new technology, new regulation, a new behavior shift — that makes this newly possible or newly necessary? (Reference case: Checkr, background-check APIs, unlocked by the gig-economy hiring boom needing background checks at a volume and speed old vendors weren't built for.) An idea with no "why now" answer is either a tar pit (someone would have done it already) or not actually new.
7. **Is there a good proxy?** A company doing something similar in an adjacent market or geography that's already working is reassuring evidence (reference case: Rappi, "food delivery for Latin America," proxied by DoorDash's success elsewhere). Proxies de-risk the "does anyone want this" question without requiring you to be first.
8. **Is this something you'd want to work on for years?** A real but softer signal — many of the best startup ideas are in "boring" spaces nobody starts out passionate about (tax software, payroll), and passion often grows with traction rather than preceding it. Don't over-weight a lukewarm answer here if the idea is otherwise strong; do flag a genuinely enthusiastic "yes" as a tailwind.
9. **Is this a scalable business?** Pure software generally passes automatically. The trap is anything that looks like software but actually requires high-skill human labor to deliver reliably — services, agencies, anything where quality or liability depends on a human doing bespoke work per customer (reference case: "AI-native service companies" claiming to replace labor can quietly become a staffing/services business if the domain requires human judgment or liability-bearing sign-off). Explicitly ask: "if this succeeds and grows 10x, does headcount grow roughly linearly with revenue?" If yes, it's a services business wearing a software costume.
10. **Is this a good idea space?** An idea space is one level of abstraction above a specific idea — a class of related ideas (e.g., "vertical SaaS for regulated industries," not just one specific product). Different idea spaces have wildly different historical hit rates (vertical SaaS and fintech infrastructure have run hot for years; consumer hardware, social networks, and ad tech have run cold) — though note explicitly that hot/cold spaces shift over time, so treat this as a directional prior, not gospel. A good idea space matters because even a wrong first idea within a good space tends to drift toward a better adjacent one (reference case: Fivetran pivoted product twice within the same idea space before landing the right one, using each failed pitch as market research).
## 3 counterintuitive signals of a GOOD idea
 
Founders systematically avoid these three properties, which is exactly why they're underpriced:
 
1. **Hard to get started (schlep blindness).** If starting requires something that looks tedious, bureaucratic, or unglamorous (a hard partnership, deep regulatory knowledge, unpleasant manual work up front), that difficulty is filtering out your competition, not warning you off (reference case: Stripe required a bank relationship and deep payments infrastructure knowledge that scared off everyone else who'd noticed the same problem).
2. **Boring space.** Founders chase ideas that sound fun to describe at a dinner party; boring-sounding spaces (payroll, compliance, logistics) get left alone for years as a result, even when the underlying problem is huge (reference case: Gusto/payroll). Note for the founder: day-to-day founder experience (writing code, fixing bugs, talking to users) ends up similar regardless of how fun the initial pitch sounded — so "boring" is a much weaker objection than it feels like early on.
3. **Existing competitors.** As in question 4 above — a crowded space with a real insight (not just "we'll do it with AI") is usually a better bet than an empty space, which more often means "no one wants this" than "untapped goldmine."
## The 7 recipes for generating ideas (roughly most-to-least reliable)
 
Use these when the founder needs raw material, not just evaluation:
 
1. **Start from your team's specific expertise.** Automatic founder-market fit. Ask the founder to walk through every job, internship, and unusual life experience and name problems they were unusually positioned to see.
2. **Start from a problem you personally, unusually, encountered.** Not a problem everyone knows about — one you saw because of a specific position (reference case: Vetcove, built by a veterinarian's sons who'd watched their father order supplies by phone for years).
3. **Think of things you personally wish existed.** Classic and productive, but the highest tar-pit risk of the seven — always immediately ask "why doesn't this already exist?" before going further.
4. **Look for recent world changes that create new opportunity.** New regulation, new technology, a sudden behavior shift (reference case: Gather Town, a pandemic-driven pivot).
5. **Find variants on recently successful companies** — geographic or vertical adaptations of a proven model (reference case: Nuvocargo, "Flexport for Latin America").
6. **Deliberately immerse in a chosen idea space and talk to people in it**, even without prior expertise — the highest-effort, highest-reliability recipe when domain expertise (recipe 1) isn't available (reference case: AtoB's founders physically visited truck stops and interviewed truckers and trucking-industry founders before choosing fuel cards as the idea).
7. **Look for large, broken industries** ripe for disruption generally.
Bonus: finding a co-founder who already has a validated idea is a legitimate way to get both at once.
 
## Closing discipline
 
Per Friedman: the only fully reliable way to know if an idea is good is to launch it and find out. Everything above is a way to stack the odds before that point — not a substitute for it. Always end an ideation session by pointing toward the cheapest real test available (usually structured discovery interviews — see `discovery-design.md`), not toward more analysis.
